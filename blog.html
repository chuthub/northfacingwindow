Advance Photo System - Type C

25 September 2025

So todays blog post is going to be about sensors. Specifically the Advanced Photo System type 'C' - or, the APS-C - sensor. The APS-C sensor is a smaller, lighter sensor used in a number of cameras that are geared to the beginner, hobbyist, or anyone looking for a small camera with a lot of photographic chops. Being smaller, it doesn't take in the same amount of light that a full-frame sensor would, and the smaller pixels produce more noise at higher ISOs. You can expect greater depth of field if you are using an APS-C sensor in your camera. My camera is a Canon M200, which is a small, mirrorless digital camera with interchangability of lenses and - you guessed it - an APS-C sensor. While this is a great sensor - especially if you fit into one of the categories above - there is one thing you need to understand to utilize it to the best of it's abilities, and that thing is what's called: Crop Factor. Since the sensor is smaller than a full-frame sensor, it accepts light slightly differently. For instance, let's look at a 35mm lens on a full-frame sensor. 35mm is one of the most commonly used focal lengths, and one of the most commonly sold prime lenses (a prime lens only has one focal length). A 35mm lens on a full-frame sensor would be equivalent to a roughly 52.5mm lens on an APS-C sensor. So what is Crop Factor? It's what determines the conversion point. For Nikon, Sony, and Fujifilm cameras, the crop factor is 1.5x. For Canon cameras it's 1.6x. In the next blog post we'll make a table to show you all the most common focal lengths and their associated conversion sizes. Until next time! Happy hunting out there with those cameras.
